1) Agentic & Governed AI

2025 marks the shift from chatty copilots to agentic systems that plan, call tools, and act with guardrails. The EU AI Act phases in through 2025–26, placing obligations on providers and users of general‑purpose and high‑risk AI, so companies are operationalising AI governance, model registries, testing, and policy controls.

SA impact: POPIA compliance, sectoral regulators, and local cloud regions support data residency. Firms deploying AI agents in finance, retail, and public services should document data flows, DPIAs, and human‑in‑the‑loop controls.

2) On‑Device & Edge AI

AI PCs and phones with NPUs bring private, low‑latency inference to endpoints, while edge compute supports factory, telco, and retail analytics. Expect hybrid patterns: run sensitive tasks on‑device, burst to cloud for heavy jobs.

SA impact: Reduced backhaul can help where connectivity is intermittent or costly. AI PCs will enter enterprise refresh cycles; plan pilots for on‑device summarisation, translation, and assistive tooling.

3) Post‑Quantum Readiness

With PQC standards finalised and timelines emerging, 2025 is about discovery and prioritisation: inventory cryptography, assess “harvest‑now, decrypt‑later” exposure, and design crypto‑agile update paths.

SA impact: Prioritise government, banking, healthcare, and telecoms systems; align with international guidance and begin migrating crypto in long‑lived systems.

4) Sustainable Compute

AI demand collides with power constraints. Enterprises scrutinise model size, inference efficiency, and carbon/water metrics; DC and cloud selection increasingly includes energy mix and PUE/WUE commitments.

SA impact: Power variability and DC growth sharpen the case for efficiency and workload placement across local and regional clouds.

5) Cybersecurity in the AI Era

Attackers industrialise with AI (faster recon, targeted phishing, evasive malware). Defenders respond with AI‑assisted detection, continuous validation (Zero Trust), and platform engineering to standardise controls via internal platforms.

SA impact: Phishing and BEC remain high‑volume threats; regulated sectors should uplift detection, MFA, and email security, and train staff for AI‑enabled social engineering.
